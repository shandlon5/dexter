Winged Woman

Rumor File — wingbeats like a storm front, shadows across the desert.

She is not a ghost and she is not an angel. Those who have seen her agree on very little except the wings — dark, membranous, fused to her arms like a bat's — and the silence that follows wherever she has been.

Sightings are rare and rarely reported. Those who do come forward describe a figure that is unmistakably female, moving low and fast, black hair hanging loose. She does not fly so much as drift — close to the ground, close to the dark.

No punctures, no trauma. Whatever she takes, she takes from the inside. Unverified accounts, people — found still, pale, and empty in a way that doctors struggle to name. The cause of death is listed as cardiac or neurological failure. It is always listed as something else.

No single culture owns this figure. A winged woman who drains the living appears in stories from Southeast Asia, Central America, and the American Southwest — separated by oceans and centuries, connected by the same hollow feeling left in her wake.

The consistent detail across cultures is not the wings. It is the hunger. And the hunger, witnesses say, is not random. She does not take indiscriminately. She comes for something specific — something she cannot generate herself. What that is, no one who has seen her up close has lived to explain in full.
